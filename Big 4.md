I reviewed each firm’s current public site across the homepage plus supporting pages for governance, ethics/code of conduct, reports or case studies, locations/contact, and accessibility. The main takeaway is that Deloitte, PwC, KPMG, and EY do not rely on brand prestige alone. Their trust comes from the same repeatable structure: a strong promise, immediate proof, and very visible accountability. ([Deloitte][1])

## What makes them feel trustworthy

* **They connect business value to professional duty.** Deloitte centers purpose and integrity, EY ties its purpose directly to trust in capital markets, PwC frames governance and transparency as foundational, and KPMG describes itself in terms of trusted advice, quality, and integrity. The messaging is ambitious, but it is always bounded by responsibility. ([Deloitte][2])

* **They explain the network honestly instead of hiding complexity.** All four explicitly explain that they are networks of separate legal entities and that the global coordinating body does not itself deliver client services or bind member firms. That kind of legal clarity is not exciting, but it is a major trust signal because it removes ambiguity about who the user is dealing with. ([Deloitte][3])

* **They put proof next to the promise.** Deloitte routes users to its Global Impact Report, stories of impact, recognition, and case studies. PwC pairs its homepage with client case studies, annual review content, governance pages, and office locations. KPMG routes users to governance, report library, code of conduct, anti-bribery, hotline, and transparency reporting. EY pairs its main narrative with case studies, newsroom content, locations, My EY, and service proof such as Digital Audit. The result is that each site gives the visitor something verifiable within a click or two. ([Deloitte][1])

* **They make accountability easy.** Deloitte repeatedly surfaces Contact Us, Submit RFP, locations, and even ethics reporting. PwC keeps office locations and contact paths prominent. KPMG repeats Contact Us and Submit RFP across core pages and also exposes newsroom and press-release routes. EY offers Connect with us, locations, newsroom media inquiries, ethics resources, and My EY. Trust increases when a visitor can quickly find a person, office, or escalation path. ([Deloitte][1])

* **They reduce global ambiguity with local routing.** Deloitte, KPMG, and EY all expose location selection or local-site controls, while PwC maintains global office and territory finders. For multinational firms, that matters: the UI immediately answers “which geography and which entity is relevant to me?” ([Deloitte][1])

* **They treat accessibility, privacy, and legal pages as trust UX, not buried compliance.** Deloitte publishes accessibility testing details and WCAG-aligned goals, PwC states active work toward WCAG 2.2 AA, KPMG describes consistent navigation, alt text, captions, and keyboard support, and EY says ey.com conforms to WCAG 2.2 AA and invites feedback on issues. That signals operational care, not just brand polish. ([Deloitte][4])

* **The UI is modular and scannable.** Across the sites, key pages are broken into repeatable blocks such as services, case studies, insights, reports, leadership, locations, and contact actions, instead of forcing users through one long wall of copy. That architecture makes the firms feel organized and methodical. This is an inference from the current page structures. ([Deloitte][1])

## What each firm emphasizes best

* **Deloitte:** strongest at action-oriented transparency. It consistently pairs brand language with next steps: contact, RFP, locations, impact report, ethics, and stories of impact. It feels reachable as well as global. ([Deloitte][1])

* **PwC:** strongest at evidence-heavy corporate credibility. Annual review, governance, board, code of conduct, case studies, and office-location infrastructure create a very boardroom-safe feel. ([PwC][5])

* **KPMG:** strongest at seriousness and compliance clarity. Its About section quickly routes to governance, reports, code, hotline, transparency, and contact/RFP actions. The tone is direct and operational. ([KPMG][6])

* **EY:** strongest at confidence language plus a modern client-experience layer. EY ties its purpose to trust in capital markets, offers My EY, keeps locations and newsroom visible, and uses pages like Digital Audit to show how trust is operationalized in services. ([EY][7])

## The guide to copy from them

The shared pattern is simple: **promise, proof, reassurance**. ([Deloitte][1])

1. **Lead with one clear outcome and one ethical anchor.**
   Your hero should say what you help clients achieve, but also what standard governs that work.
   Good pattern: “We help [audience] achieve [outcome] through [capability], backed by [quality / independence / integrity].”

2. **Put proof directly under the hero.**
   Do not make users search for credibility. Show case studies, annual report, governance page, client results, or a named methodology immediately below the headline.

3. **Design the navigation around trust questions.**
   A professional-services site should quickly answer:

   * What do you do?
   * Who do you help?
   * Why should I believe you?
   * Who are you, exactly?
   * How do I contact the right team?

   A good top nav is usually: **Services | Industries | Case Studies or Insights | About | Locations | Contact**

4. **Expose governance and standards early.**
   Do not hide structure, code of conduct, quality approach, independence, or reporting channels in obscure legal pages. Serious buyers read those.

5. **Make a human easy to reach.**
   Keep **Contact us**, **Talk to an expert**, **Submit RFP**, or an office finder visible in the header or persistent footer.

6. **Use calm, precise language.**
   These firms rarely sound playful or overly clever in their trust pages. The tone is measured, specific, and low-drama. That helps more than flashy copy.

7. **Build for global-local clarity.**
   If you operate across markets, include location selection, local office pages, and clear entity cues so users know which team or jurisdiction applies.

8. **Treat accessibility, privacy, and legal pages as part of brand quality.**
   A trustworthy site makes those pages easy to find and easy to understand.

## A homepage model based on this review

A strong homepage for this category would look like this:

**Hero** → clear business promise + ethical/quality anchor
**Proof strip** → case studies, annual report, governance, client results
**Services/industries** → scannable modules
**Insight/report block** → current research or thought leadership
**Locations/contact** → office finder + expert contact + RFP
**Trust footer** → code of conduct, governance, privacy, accessibility, report a concern

The reason these sites work is that they are easy to verify. Almost every major claim has an adjacent path to confirm it: a report, a case study, a governance page, an office locator, or a contact route. That is the core trust lesson to copy.
